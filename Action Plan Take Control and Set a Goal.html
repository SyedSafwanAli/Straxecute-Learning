Action Plan: Take Control and Set a Goal

The next step in charting a career path is to take your vision statement and set goals that are truly based on your interests and talents, taking into account where you want to go, what you like and dislike about your specific position or field, and what an ideal career would look like. Most importantly, you should think about the “why’s” associated with each of these goals – are they motivated by your passions or something else?

Goal Setting 101:

A few things to keep in mind as you begin the process of setting career goals:

  • Short-term goals are completed within a few weeks to a few months.
  • Mid-range goals are completed over a few months to a year (or two at the most). They often will have some short-term goals associated with them.
  • Long-term goals usually take several years to achieve and have both short-term and mid-range goals associated with them. Additionally, goals must have the following things associated with them.

Additionally, goals must have the following things associated with them:

  • Actions, which may or may not be the same as your short-term goals. They are the activities you’ll engage in as you work toward your goal.
  • Checkpoints, which will help you to know if you’re staying on track. They could include performance reviews and selfassessments.
  • Achievements, which are spots along the way that will let you know you’re succeeding. They could include receiving an award or earning a promotion.

Your career development should feature a mix of all these kinds of goals, and include milestones where necessary. Identifying the actions to take to achieve your goals will be a key element. Remember to celebrate the achievements, as they’ll keep you motivated toward achieving that long-term goal.

On the Game Pages in the Playbook, you’ll have an opportunity to answer some of the questions that should help you begin forming your career goals. Once you have a goal in mind, start thinking about the actions, checkpoints, and achievements you will go through to reach it.

Example:

Here’s what one strong long-term career goal might look like, as well as some shorter-term actions, checkpoints, and achievements that would be associated with it.

Goal:

Within the next five years, I want to become a manager within my company’s Program Management Office in San Diego

Actions:
  • Participate in regular mentoring or some job-shadowing on the functions in the office
  • Work with my current boss (and a program manager, if possible) to take on more team and project leadership responsibilities
  • Actively pursue management opportunities – check our Careers
  • Research apartments in the San Diego area, and moving costs
Checkpoints:
  • Seek regular feedback from managers who oversee my projects or work
  • Find a leadership coach and set up monthly coaching sessions
  • Prepare for the PgMP Certification test (next January)
Achievements:
  • Become PgMP certified
  • Earn a promotion to management within three years (in either the desired position or a similar one)

Remember, your goals don’t have to look just like this. Just make sure that your goals represent a future vision that the present-day you isn’t yet prepared for, that they account for the skills and experience that you’re missing, and that they clearly state a rough plan for how you can close those gaps and realize your dream. You may not be able to be super specific right now, but you can at least start moving in the right direction.

One word of caution though… As Jack has said numerous times, don’t ever let your career ambitions overshadow your current performance. It’s great to have goals. We all should have them. They help us focus and they keep us motivated, but when the pursuit of these goals becomes more focused on “your next move” rather than overdelivering in your current role, that is a sure sign that you’re approaching your career development from the wrong perspective.
